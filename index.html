Psychoacoustic Tonality: Models and Their Accuracy in Vehicle Cabin Noise

By Zhenxian Li  ·  Li Acoustics  ·  December 2025
Psychoacoustic Tonality
Tonal components (blue) rising above a broadband noise floor (grey); $\Delta L$ is the level excess used in tonality assessment.
DomainPsychoacoustics, NVH
Key metricTonality $K$ or $T$
StandardsECMA-418-2, ISO 1996
ApplicationsEV/ICEV cabin noise, product sound quality

Psychoacoustic tonality is a perceptual measure of how strongly tonal components — pure tones, narrow-band harmonics, or tonal complexes — stand out from an otherwise broadband or noisy sound. A sound with high tonality is one in which the listener clearly perceives a discrete pitch or whistle above the background noise. Because tonal noise is often rated as more annoying than broadband noise at the same overall sound pressure level, tonality has become a key metric in product sound quality engineering and in environmental noise regulation.

This article introduces the concept of tonality, describes the principal tone-prominence descriptors used in engineering practice, and compares two widely used computational models: the empirical approach developed by Aures (1985), and the hearing-model-based approach standardised in ECMA-418-2. The comparison is made in the context of electric vehicle (EV) and internal-combustion-engine vehicle (ICEV) cabin noise, where tonality assessment presents specific challenges.

1. What is psychoacoustic tonality?

In psychoacoustics, tonality refers to the perceptual salience of tonal components embedded in an otherwise broadband sound. Two signals with similar overall level can differ strongly in perceived tonality depending on three factors:

  • how much energy is concentrated into audible tonal components;
  • how strongly those components are masked by neighbouring spectral content;
  • how the auditory system integrates information across time and across critical bands.

Tonal noise is often perceived as more annoying than broadband noise at the same sound pressure level, a finding supported by numerous listening studies. Consequently, quantifying tonality is important both in product sound quality assessment and in environmental noise control. In engineering practice, tonality metrics aim to convert this perceptual attribute into a reproducible quantity that can be compared across operating conditions — for instance, across different engine orders or electric-motor tonal components — and across design iterations.

2. Tone prominence descriptors

Before introducing full tonality models, it is useful to distinguish local tone prominence descriptors from global tonality metrics. Both attempt to characterise how much a tonal component stands out from its background, but they do so at different levels of complexity.

2.1 Tone-to-noise ratio (TNR)

The most direct descriptor is the tone-to-noise ratio (TNR), which compares the level of a candidate tonal component $L_\mathrm{tone}$ with an estimate of the surrounding background level $L_\mathrm{bg}$ derived from adjacent frequency groups or critical bands:

$$\mathrm{TNR} \approx L_\mathrm{tone} - L_\mathrm{bg} \quad [\mathrm{dB}]$$

A larger TNR indicates a more salient tonal component. A high TNR means the tone rises well above the local noise floor and is likely to be audible as a discrete component.

2.2 Prominence ratio (PR)

Prominence ratio (PR) follows a similar idea but uses a critical-band representation and constructs the background estimate from neighbouring bands. If the energy in the critical band containing the tone is sufficiently larger than that in the adjacent bands, the component is classified as prominent. Both TNR and PR are included in international standards (notably ISO 1996-2) as objective indicators of tonal prominence and are useful for locating and ranking tonal components.

2.3 Limitations of simple descriptors

Despite their simplicity, TNR and PR are not complete tonality models. They do not explicitly account for how multiple tones combine, how the overall signal-to-noise situation modulates perceived tonality, or how results should be calibrated onto a perceptual scale. Because they rely mainly on spectral level differences, they do not fully represent psychoacoustic phenomena such as absolute audibility, masking, and the interaction between time and frequency resolution.

For example, a tone that barely exceeds the local background may yield a similar TNR at low and high absolute sound levels, even though its perceptual salience can differ markedly. Likewise, high spectral resolution requires long analysis windows, which may under-represent short-lived or rapidly varying tonal events. These limitations motivated the development of more complete psychoacoustic tonality models.

3. Tonality models

Two principal model families are compared here. The Aures (1985) family starts from identified tonal components and combines them through empirical weighting functions into a single tonality estimate. The ECMA-418-2 hearing-model approach operates on auditory-filtered signals and uses autocorrelation-based periodicity analysis within critical bands to distinguish tonal from non-tonal components.

3.1 Aures' tonality model (Klanghaftigkeit)

Aures' model treats tonality — denoted Klanghaftigkeit, symbol $K$ — as the combination of two contributions: (a) a tonal-component contribution derived from all audible tonal components in the spectrum, and (b) a noise-loudness contribution that penalises situations where noise dominates the overall loudness.

For each audible tonal component $k$ with level excess $\Delta L_k > 0$ above its local background, centre frequency $f_k$, and effective bandwidth in Bark $\Delta z_k$, Aures introduces three psychoacoustically motivated weighting functions. A frequency-dependent weight $w_2(f)$, band-pass shaped around the region most relevant for pitch perception:

$$w_2(f)=\left(\frac{1}{1+0.2\left(\dfrac{0.7\,\mathrm{kHz}}{f}-\dfrac{f}{0.7\,\mathrm{kHz}}\right)^2}\right)^{0.29}$$

A saturation-type weight for level excess — reflecting the fact that perceived tonality grows quickly at first but eventually saturates:

$$w_3(\Delta L)=1-\exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta L}{15\,\mathrm{dB}}\right)$$

Together with a bandwidth-related weight $w_1(\Delta z)$, the overall tonal weight is computed as a quadratic mean over all tonal components:

$$W_T = \sqrt{\sum_k \left[w_1'(\Delta z_k)\,w_2'(f_k)\,w_3'(\Delta L_k)\right]^2}$$

To represent the dominance of tonal over noise energy, the model computes the loudness of the noise-only part (tonal components removed), $N_G$, and compares it to the total loudness $N$:

$$W_{QT}=1-\frac{N_G}{N}$$

Finally, tonality is obtained by combining both contributions with a calibration constant $c$ and empirical exponents:

$$K \;=\; c\; W_T^{0.29}\; W_{QT}^{0.79}$$

where $c$ is chosen such that a reference 1 kHz pure tone at a prescribed level yields $K = 1$. The exponents reflect the compressive nature of tonality perception: large increases in tonal strength produce only moderate increases in perceived tonality, much like the decibel scale itself compresses the enormous range of human hearing into a manageable number.

Improved Aures variants

Recent work (Estreder et al., 2023) revisits Aures' procedure for complex sounds by refining spectral resolution, tonal-detection criteria, and the exponent structure. In this framework, the noise-loudness contribution is expressed explicitly as a loudness weighting:

$$W_L = 1 - \frac{L_N}{L_S}$$

where $L_N$ is the loudness level of the signal after removing detected tonal components and $L_S$ is the loudness level of the full signal frame. The frame-wise tonality estimate becomes:

$$\ell_m = \min\!\left(C_{1\mathrm{k},60}\,W_T^{0.29}\,W_L^{0.79},\;1\right)$$

with $C_{1\mathrm{k},60}$ calibrated so that a 1 kHz, 60 dB SPL pure tone yields $\ell_m = 1$. These refinements preserve the basic Aures structure while improving agreement with listening tests for dense harmonic spectra and time-varying conditions typical of EV motor noise.

3.2 Hearing-model-based tonality (ECMA-418-2)

A more recent and comprehensive approach is standardised in ECMA-418-2 and implemented in commercial tools such as the HEAD acoustics "Tonality (Hearing Model)" analysis. Rather than starting from explicit spectral tone extraction alone, this method simulates key stages of human auditory processing and operates on a critical-band representation.

The key step is estimating a normalised autocorrelation function $\varphi_{l,z}(m)$ in each auditory band $z$ and time block $l$. Intuitively, a periodic or tonal signal produces a pronounced autocorrelation peak at its period, whereas random noise does not — the model exploits this difference to separate tonal from non-tonal energy in the loudness domain.

Tonal candidates obtained from autocorrelation peaks are mapped to an estimated specific loudness of the tonal component $N'_\mathrm{tonal}(l,z)$. The specific loudness of the noise component in each band is then:

$$N'_\mathrm{noise}(l,z) = N'_\mathrm{signal}(l,z)\,h_\mathrm{LP}(l) - N'_\mathrm{tonal}(l,z)$$

An overall loudness signal-to-noise ratio is computed across all bands:

$$\mathrm{SNR}(l) = \frac{\displaystyle\max_{z}\, N'_\mathrm{tonal}(l,z)}{\varepsilon + \displaystyle\sum_{z} N'_\mathrm{noise}(l,z)}$$

A nonlinear scaling factor $q(l)$, with parameters $A$ and $B$ specified by the standard, compresses this SNR into a perceptually meaningful range:

$$q(l) = \begin{cases} 1-\exp\!\big(-A\,(\mathrm{SNR}(l)-B)\big), & \text{if } \exp\!\big(-A\,(\mathrm{SNR}(l)-B)\big) < 1, \\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}$$

The time-dependent specific tonality in each band is then:

$$T'(l,z) = c_T\,q(l)\,N'_\mathrm{tonal}(l,z)$$

where $c_T$ is a calibration factor. An overall time-varying tonality is the maximum across bands:

$$T(l) = \max_{z}\, T'(l,z)$$

A single representative value $T$ is obtained by time-averaging $T(l)$ over frames where tonality exceeds a small threshold, discarding the initial transient.

Because this approach works in the loudness domain within an explicit auditory model, it naturally accounts for psychoacoustic effects that simpler models handle only approximately: absolute audibility (through the hearing threshold), level dependence (through the nonlinear loudness map), and fine temporal resolution (through running autocorrelation). The output can be represented as a single scalar or as tonality-versus-time and tonality-versus-frequency information.

4. Comparison and discussion

The two model families share the broad goal of quantifying tonality on a perceptually calibrated scale, but differ fundamentally in their signal representations and psychoacoustic assumptions.

The Aures family operates in the spectral domain: it identifies tonal components explicitly and combines them through empirical weighting functions. This makes the model transparent and computationally lightweight, but means its accuracy depends on the quality of the tone extraction step. For complex spectra with overlapping components or rapidly changing operating conditions — such as EV motor orders sweeping in frequency — the explicit extraction step can introduce errors.

The ECMA-418-2 hearing model operates in the loudness and autocorrelation domains. It does not need to identify individual tonal components before computing tonality; instead, periodicity and tonality emerge jointly from the auditory model. This makes it more robust for complex and time-varying sounds, at the cost of greater computational complexity and reduced interpretability.

For EV cabin noise in particular, ECMA-418-2 is generally regarded as the more appropriate tool, because EV powertrains generate prominent, order-tracking tonal components over a wide speed range in an otherwise relatively quiet background. However, the improved Aures formulation of Estreder et al. (2023) has narrowed this gap by addressing the spectral resolution and weighting limitations of the original 1985 model.
