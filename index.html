How Do We Measure Whether a Sound Is "Tonal"?

Inside the psychoacoustic models that quantify tonality — and why getting this right matters for electric vehicles.

ZL
Zhenxian Li
December 2025  |  12 min read

When you sit inside an electric vehicle and notice a faint, persistent whine from the motor, your brain immediately picks it out from the background hum — even if, in pure decibels, it is no louder. That ability to perceive a tonal component embedded in broadband noise is the subject of psychoacoustic tonality, and engineering it correctly has become a central challenge in modern vehicle acoustics.

This article explains what tonality is, how it is measured, and how two leading computational models approach the problem differently.

What is tonality?

Tonal components (blue) rising above a broadband noise floor; $\Delta L$ is the level excess driving tonality.

In psychoacoustics, tonality describes how strongly a listener perceives discrete pitch-like components in a sound — components that stand out above the surrounding noise. Two sounds can have identical overall loudness and yet feel completely different: one might be heard as a neutral roar, the other as an irritating whine.

Three factors govern this perception:

  • how much energy is concentrated in narrow spectral components;
  • how strongly those components are masked by neighbouring noise;
  • how the auditory system integrates information across time and across critical bands.

Tonal noise is consistently rated as more annoying than broadband noise at the same sound pressure level. This makes tonality a key metric in product sound quality engineering and in planning-law noise assessments, where regulators may require a penalty to be added to measured levels if tonal components are detected.

Tone prominence descriptors

Before tackling full perceptual models, engineers typically use simpler descriptors to locate and rank individual tonal components. These are fast to compute and well-suited to automated screening.

Tone-to-noise ratio

The tone-to-noise ratio (TNR) compares the level of a candidate tonal component $L_\mathrm{tone}$ to an estimate of the surrounding background $L_\mathrm{bg}$ derived from adjacent frequency groups:

$$\mathrm{TNR} \approx L_\mathrm{tone} - L_\mathrm{bg} \quad [\mathrm{dB}]$$

A large TNR means the tone rises well above its local noise floor and is likely audible as a discrete pitch.

Prominence ratio

Prominence ratio (PR) follows the same idea but works in a critical-band representation. If the energy in the band containing the tone substantially exceeds that in the neighbouring bands, the component is flagged as prominent. Both TNR and PR appear in international standards (notably ISO 1996-2) as objective indicators.

Limitations

A tone barely exceeding the local noise floor can yield similar TNR values at very different absolute sound levels — yet its perceived salience can differ dramatically. Simple level-difference descriptors miss this.

TNR and PR are not complete tonality models. They handle tones in isolation and cannot account for how multiple tones combine, how absolute hearing thresholds affect audibility, or how the total noise energy modulates the perception of each tonal component. These limitations motivated the development of full psychoacoustic tonality models.

Two tonality models

Two families of models dominate engineering practice. The Aures (1985) approach starts from identified spectral components and combines them through empirically derived weighting functions. The ECMA-418-2 approach simulates auditory processing directly, using autocorrelation to detect periodicity within critical bands.

Aures' model (1985)

Aures' model, named after its German term Klanghaftigkeit (symbol $K$), decomposes tonality into two contributions: the prominence of individual tonal components, and the degree to which the overall signal is dominated by tonal rather than noise energy.

For each audible tonal component $k$ with level excess $\Delta L_k > 0$ above its background, centre frequency $f_k$, and bandwidth in Bark $\Delta z_k$, three weighting functions are applied. A frequency weight $w_2(f)$ — band-pass shaped, reflecting that the ear is most sensitive to tonal components in the mid-frequency range:

$$w_2(f)=\left(\frac{1}{1+0.2\!\left(\dfrac{0.7\,\mathrm{kHz}}{f}-\dfrac{f}{0.7\,\mathrm{kHz}}\right)^{\!2}}\right)^{\!0.29}$$

A level-excess weight $w_3(\Delta L)$ — saturating, because very large $\Delta L$ values do not produce proportionally larger increases in perceived tonality:

$$w_3(\Delta L)=1-\exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta L}{15\,\mathrm{dB}}\right)$$

Together with a bandwidth weight $w_1(\Delta z)$, the combined tonal weight is a quadratic mean across all components:

$$W_T = \sqrt{\sum_k \left[w_1'(\Delta z_k)\,w_2'(f_k)\,w_3'(\Delta L_k)\right]^2}$$

The noise-dominance factor compares the loudness of the noise-only signal (tonal components removed), $N_G$, to the total loudness $N$:

$$W_{QT}=1-\frac{N_G}{N}$$

Both contributions are combined into a single tonality value:

$$K \;=\; c\; W_T^{0.29}\; W_{QT}^{0.79}$$

The calibration constant $c$ is set so that a 1 kHz reference tone at a prescribed level gives $K = 1$.

Improved variants (Estreder et al., 2023)

A recent refinement replaces the noise-dominance factor with an explicit loudness weighting, using loudness levels $L_N$ (noise only) and $L_S$ (full signal):

$$W_L = 1 - \frac{L_N}{L_S}, \qquad \ell_m = \min\!\left(C_{1\mathrm{k},60}\,W_T^{0.29}\,W_L^{0.79},\;1\right)$$

These adjustments improve agreement with listening tests for dense harmonic spectra — exactly the kind generated by EV motor orders sweeping across a wide speed range.

ECMA-418-2 hearing model

The ECMA-418-2 model, implemented commercially in tools like HEAD acoustics' "Tonality (Hearing Model)", takes a fundamentally different approach: instead of extracting tones first and weighting them afterwards, it simulates the auditory system's own processing.

The key insight is that a periodic signal produces a pronounced peak in its autocorrelation function at its period, whereas random noise produces no such peak. By computing a normalised autocorrelation $\varphi_{l,z}(m)$ in each critical band $z$ and time block $l$, the model separates tonal from non-tonal energy directly in the loudness domain.

Tonal components are mapped to a specific tonal loudness $N'_\mathrm{tonal}(l,z)$; the residual noise loudness per band is:

$$N'_\mathrm{noise}(l,z) = N'_\mathrm{signal}(l,z)\,h_\mathrm{LP}(l) - N'_\mathrm{tonal}(l,z)$$

An overall loudness signal-to-noise ratio across all bands then modulates the tonality estimate:

$$\mathrm{SNR}(l) = \frac{\displaystyle\max_{z}\, N'_\mathrm{tonal}(l,z)}{\varepsilon + \displaystyle\sum_{z} N'_\mathrm{noise}(l,z)}$$

A nonlinear scaling factor $q(l)$, with constants $A$ and $B$ specified by the standard, compresses this SNR into a perceptually calibrated range. The final time–frequency tonality map is:

$$T'(l,z) = c_T\,q(l)\,N'_\mathrm{tonal}(l,z)$$

An overall time-varying tonality is the maximum across bands, $T(l) = \max_z T'(l,z)$, and a single representative value $T$ is obtained by time-averaging over frames exceeding a small threshold.

Comparison

The two models share the goal of producing a perceptually calibrated tonality value, but their differences matter in practice.

Aures' model is transparent and interpretable: you can trace every step from the identified tonal components through the weighting functions to the final $K$ value. This makes it useful for engineering diagnosis. Its weakness is that accuracy depends on the tone-extraction step — a step that can fail for complex, overlapping harmonic spectra.

ECMA-418-2 is more robust for complex and time-varying sounds. Because periodicity and tonality are estimated jointly within an auditory model, the method handles situations where classical spectral extraction would struggle: rapidly sweeping motor orders, tones close to the hearing threshold, or multiple harmonics interacting with masking noise. The trade-off is greater computational cost and a less intuitive signal chain.

For EV cabin noise — with its prominent, speed-tracking motor orders in an otherwise quiet background — ECMA-418-2 is generally the more appropriate choice. The improved Aures formulation of Estreder et al. (2023) has narrowed the gap, but the hearing-model approach remains the current benchmark.
